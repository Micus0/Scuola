### 3. DISCUSS the following questions

1.  **Physical and Psychological Suffering:** Owen portrays physical suffering through vivid, grotesque imagery: soldiers are **"bent double, like old beggars"** and **"blood-shod"** (walking in their own blood). Psychological trauma is represented by the speaker’s **"smothering dreams"**, where he is haunted by the **"helpless sight"** of his comrade's agonizing death.
2.  **The World at the end of Stanza 2:** The poem introduces a **surreal, underwater-like world**. Seen through the green goggles of a gas mask, the atmosphere becomes a **"green sea"** where the victim appears to be **"drowning"** in the toxic mist.
3.  **Theme and Message:** The central theme is the **brutality and futility of modern warfare**. The message is a fierce denunciation of **"The Old Lie"**—the romanticized idea that dying for one's country is sweet and noble. Owen exposes the gap between patriotic propaganda and the horrific reality of the trenches.
4.  **Owen’s Quote:** The quote is highly relevant. Owen acts as a **"pleader"** for his men. By documenting their "froth-corrupted lungs" and "vile, incurable sores," he forces the reader to confront the human cost of war that official reports often ignore.


### 4. WRITE: Owen vs. Brooke (10-12 Lines)

In contrast to **Rupert Brooke’s "The Soldier,"** which presents a **patriotic and idealistic** view of war, Owen’s poem is **bitterly realistic**. Brooke describes death as a peaceful return to an "eternal mind" within a "foreign field" that becomes England; his mood is serene and sacrificial. Owen, however, focuses on the **physical decay and agony** of the dying, using harsh imagery like **"obscene as cancer."** While Brooke’s attitude is one of noble duty, Owen’s is one of **outraged protest**. Brooke’s imagery is lyrical and pastoral, whereas Owen’s is sensory and violent. Ultimately, Brooke’s message is that war is a glorious sacrifice, while Owen’s message is that war is a **senseless slaughter** that destroys the youth of a nation.


### 5. TIME FOR ORACY: Talk Outline

*   **Introduction:** Modern war is often experienced through photography and film. These visuals provide an immediate, visceral shock.
*   **Techniques:** Photography shows the **external horror** (blood, ruins, and bodies). Poetry, like Owen's, uses **onomatopoeia** ("gargling") and **metaphor** ("bitter as the cud") to make us "feel" the internal pain and the lasting psychological trauma.
*   **Conclusion:** While images are more immediate, **poetry is often more effective** for long-term impact. It forces the audience to use their imagination, making the horror of war more personal, haunting, and unforgettable.
