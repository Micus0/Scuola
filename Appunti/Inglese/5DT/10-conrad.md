### The horror

**Summary**

The text chronicles the final moments and death of Kurtz, the ivory agent, as witnessed by the narrator, Marlow. Despite his physical decay, Kurtz remains a figure of immense psychological complexity. He is haunted by memories of his grand ambitions and his descent into moral darkness. 

As the steamer progresses through the "grimy fragment of another world," Kurtz’s health fails completely. In a final moment of intense lucidity and "complete knowledge," he experiences a vision of his own life and actions, whispering his final judgment: **"The horror! The horror!"** Following his death, announced with disdain by the manager's servant (*"Mistah Kurtz—he dead"*), Marlow reflects on Kurtz's legacy, concluding that despite his atrocities, Kurtz was a "remarkable man" because he dared to face the truth of his own soul.


**Detailed Analysis**

**A. The Character of Kurtz: A "Hollow Sham"**

The text explores the internal void left by Kurtz’s abandonment of civilization.
*   **"Hollow sham":** This phrase suggests that Kurtz's outer eloquence and noble ideals were merely a shell; underneath, there was no moral core to resist the influence of the wilderness.
*   **"Diabolic love and unearthly hate":** These opposing forces represent the psychological extremes Kurtz reached, showing a man who has transcended human morality into something monstrous.
*   **"Contemptibly childish":** Marlow notes the irony that a man so feared and powerful could also be reduced to "childish" fantasies of being greeted by kings at a railway station.


**B. The Symbolism of Darkness and the Wilderness**

Darkness is not just a lack of light, but a spiritual state.
*   **"Impenetrable darkness":** Kurtz is described as lying at the bottom of a precipice where the sun never shines, symbolizing his total isolation from humanity and grace.
*   **"Invisible wilderness":** The wilderness is portrayed as an active force that Kurtz "wrestled" with, eventually penetrating his soul.
*   **"Barren darkness":** Unlike the fertile jungle, the darkness within Kurtz is sterile and leads only to death.


**C. "The Horror!": The Moment of Truth**

Kurtz’s final words are perhaps the most famous in English literature.
*   **"Sombre pride, ruthless power, craven terror":** These emotions flash across Kurtz's face in his final seconds, showing a man reviewing his life's work and realizing its true nature.
*   **"A cry that was no more than a breath":** The physical weakness of the cry emphasizes the internal, spiritual weight of his realization.
*   **"The last opportunity for pronouncement":** Marlow admires Kurtz because, unlike the "pellegrini" (the mediocre company men), Kurtz had the courage to sum up the universe and judge it, even if that judgment was negative.


**D. Marlow’s Perspective: "Brutally Callous"**

Marlow’s reaction to the death highlights his loyalty to Kurtz over the Company.
*   **"Mistah Kurtz—he dead":** The casual, contemptuous way the boy announces the death contrasts with Marlow's profound internal experience.
*   **"I was considered brutally callous":** Marlow refuses to join the others in their superficial mourning or curiosity, choosing instead to protect Kurtz's memory and "papers" from the "manager," whom he distrusts.
*   **"Victory":** Marlow views Kurtz's final cry as a "moral victory" because it was a statement of truth in a world of lies and hypocrisy.
