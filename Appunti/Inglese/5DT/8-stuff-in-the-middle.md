# English literature
## The Bronte sisters

* **Early Life & Education**: Charlotte, Emily, and Anne were the daughters of an **Anglican clergyman** of Irish origin. They lived in isolation in **Yorkshire** and were primarily **self-educated** by reading in their father's library.
* **Pseudonyms**: To avoid the gender bias of the 19th century, they published their work under **male pen names**: **Currer** (Charlotte), **Ellis** (Emily), and **Acton** (Anne) **Bell**.
* **Literary Breakthrough (1847)**: Each sister published a major novel that year: Charlotte’s ***Jane Eyre*** (an immediate success), Emily’s ***Wuthering Heights***, and Anne’s ***Agnes Grey***.
* **Tragic Deaths**: Emily and Anne died of **consumption** (tuberculosis) shortly after their novels were published (1848–1849). Charlotte died in 1855, likely due to pregnancy-related illness.

### Jane Eyre by Charlotte

Plot

* **Early Years**: Jane is a penniless **orphan** raised by her cold aunt, Mrs Reed. She is later sent to **Lowood School**, a very strict institution where she faces harsh conditions.
* **Thornfield Hall**: Jane becomes a **governess** at Thornfield Hall and falls in love with its owner, **Mr Rochester**. Her stay is marked by mysterious and frightening events.
* **The Secret**: During their wedding, a stranger reveals that Rochester is already married to **Bertha Mason**, a "madwoman" hidden in the attic. Heartbroken, Jane flees and finds refuge at **Moor House** with St John Rivers.
* **Resolution**: After hearing Rochester’s voice calling her, Jane returns to find Thornfield destroyed by a fire started by Bertha. **Mr Rochester**, now blind and injured, lives in **Ferndean**; Jane marries him, and they find happiness together.


Settings

* **Gateshead**: The Reeds' home and the place of Jane's **childhood**. The name stands for 'gateway', representing her unhappiest moments and her ethical awakening.
* **Lowood School**: The place of Jane's **education**. The name means 'low wood', symbolizing a 'low' time in her life while being located in a low valley next to a wood.
* **Thornfield**: Mr Rochester's house, the place of **independence and young love**. It is an allegorical name meaning a 'field of thorns', a place of mystery and temptation.
* **Moor House**: The Rivers' house on the moor, the place of **temporary banishment**. Located in the wilderness, it is where Jane tries to give a sense to her life again.
* **Ferndean**: Mr Rochester's rural mansion, the place for a **new start**. Meaning 'fern hill', it represents a 'new Eden' where Jane finally finds mature love.


Characters

* **Jane Eyre**: She is an intense, imaginative, and rebellious heroine who fights for recognition and self-respect. She represents the "outsider" and the free spirit struggling against a class-ridden and money-oriented Victorian society.
* **Mr Rochester**: A complex figure who transforms from a "lustful villain" into a "Byronic hero." Unlike a stereotypical seducer, he is a nobleman of passion attracted to Jane's soul and personality rather than her appearance.
* **Bertha Mason**: Rochester’s insane Creole wife. She is depicted through the "Gothic" lens as a monster or a vampire, serving as a representation of Victorian prejudice against other ethnic groups due to her mixed-race origins.
* **Symbolic Development**: Each section of the novel represents a new phase in Jane’s development, reflecting her inner conflicts between spirit and flesh, duty and desire, and her ultimate journey toward fulfillment.


Themes

* **Bildungsroman (Novel of Growing Up)**: The novel focuses on Jane's development, where the themes of childhood and education are central to her journey.
* **Search for Love and Recognition**: Jane seeks to be loved and respected as a human being deserving of affection, rather than just in a romantic sense.
* **Autonomy and Integrity**: As she matures, Jane gains economic independence and twice refuses marriage proposals to protect her moral integrity and ensure a relationship between equals.
* **Social Criticism**: Charlotte Brontë criticises the strict Victorian social class system. Despite her education, Jane's position as a governess places her in a social limbo where she is treated like a servant.
* **Gothic Elements**: The story uses traditional Gothic conventions, such as childhood terrors and mysterious, threatening sounds that foreshadow the tragedy at Thornfield.


Style

* **Heroine as Narrator**: The use of Jane as the narrator provides unity to the novel. Everything is seen through her point of view, allowing the author to identify with her and engage the reader.
* **First-Person Narrative**: The story is told in the first person. Jane frequently addresses the reader directly to explain her feelings and decision-making process.
* **Emotional Use of Language**: The language is straightforward but adapts to the style and mood of each character. This emotional tone reflects the author's focus on the nature of human relationships.
* **Motifs and Symbols**: The text features recurring symbols and images, such as supernatural elements, important dreams, patterns of light and dark, and the opposition between warmth and cold.


A woman's standpoint (key idea)

* **A New Voice**: Jane Eyre introduced a new quality to the English novel: the voice of a woman speaking about herself with perfect frankness.
* **Passionate Love**: The novel describes passionate love from a woman's standpoint, which shocked many readers at the time.
* **Realism vs. Romance**: Unlike traditional heroines who were expected to be beautiful and rich, Jane Eyre is "moderately plain," making her a more realistic character.
* **Unconventional Plot**: The story breaks social norms as the heroine falls in love with a man who is not only rich but also already married to a "mad wife."

### Bertha Mason (56)

The passage describes the climax where **Mr. Rochester** takes Jane and two witnesses to the attic of **Thornfield Hall** to reveal his secret: his first wife, **Bertha Mason**, is alive and imprisoned. Inside a "**room without a window**," they find a violent, dehumanized woman guarded by Grace Poole. After Bertha attacks Rochester, he bitterly compares this "**maniac**" to the "quiet and quiescent" Jane, explaining the "subtle doom" that has just destroyed Jane's wedding day.

#### **Dehumanization and the "Gothic Monster"**
Brontë uses intense Gothic imagery to strip Bertha of her humanity, presenting her as a creature of pure impulse.
*   **"A figure ran backwards and forwards"**: Bertha is initially described as an indistinct "figure" rather than a person.
*   **"It snatched and growled"**: The use of the pronoun "**it**" instead of "she" suggests she has lost her human identity through madness.
*   **"The clothed hyena"**: This metaphor (line 20) reinforces her predatory, savage nature.
*   **"Quantity of dark, grizzled hair, wild as a mane"**: Her appearance is explicitly linked to that of a wild animal.

#### **The Symbolic Contrast: Jane vs. Bertha**
Rochester uses Bertha as a "foil" to justify his love for Jane, creating a sharp binary between the two women.
*   **"That bulk" vs. "This young girl"**: He points to Bertha’s physical mass to highlight Jane’s perceived purity and fragility.
*   **"Fierce ragout" vs. "Pure, fresh, healthy"**: He compares Bertha to a spicy, over-seasoned dish (associated with sin/madness) while describing Jane’s soul as clean.
*   **"Red balls yonder"**: Refers to Bertha’s bloodshot eyes, contrasting them with Jane’s "clear eyes."

#### **Setting and Atmosphere (The "Subtle Doom")**
The environment reflects Jane's internal emotional state.
*   **"A Christmas frost had come at midsummer"**: This powerful metaphor symbolizes the sudden death of Jane's hopes; her "blooming" wedding day is instantly frozen by the truth.
*   **"A subtle doom"**: Jane feels her future has been struck down unexpectedly, comparing her situation to the biblical plague of the first-born in Egypt.

#### **Social and Colonial Themes**
*   **"A woman, Bertha Mason, from Jamaica"**: Her origin is significant. In Victorian literature, madness was often associated with the heat and "savagery" of colonial territories.
*   **"The maniac bellowed"**: Her lack of coherent speech symbolizes her total loss of status and agency within Victorian society.

#### **The Supernatural in *Jane Eyre*** (key idea)

*   **Gothic & Realism**: Charlotte Brontë uses supernatural imagery to create a Gothic atmosphere within a realistic social context.
*   **Rational Explanations**: Most "paranormal" events are explained logically; for example, the mysterious noises at Thornfield are revealed to be **Bertha Mason**, Rochester’s hidden wife.
*   **Jane vs. Bertha**: Bertha mirrors Jane's own **repressed anxieties**. While Bertha is physically locked in an attic, Jane is socially imprisoned by her poverty, class, and gender.
*   **Longing for Freedom**: Both women crave liberty. Bertha escapes to take revenge, while Jane seeks independence as Rochester tries to control her.
*   **The Turning Point**: At the end, a truly supernatural event occurs: Jane hears Rochester’s voice calling her from miles away. This "irrational" moment leads her to choose her own path and true life with him.

### Wide Sargasso sea (Jean Rhys)

**Jean Rhys (1894–1979)**

*   **Background**: Born in the **West Indies**, she moved to Britain at sixteen. Her mixed heritage gave her a unique perspective on the colonial relationship between the Caribbean and England.
*   **Motivation**: Rhys felt that Charlotte Brontë’s portrayal of Bertha Mason as a "mad monster" was biased. She wrote *Wide Sargasso Sea* (1966) to give the character a voice, a name (**Antoinette Cosway**), and a tragic human history.


**Plot of *Wide Sargasso Sea***

The novel acts as a **prequel** to *Jane Eyre* and is divided into three parts:
*   **Part One**: Describes Antoinette’s childhood in **Jamaica**, marked by poverty, racial tension, and her mother’s mental breakdown.
*   **Part Two**: Details her marriage to a young Englishman (Rochester). He is overwhelmed by the **sensuality** and "otherness" of the island and eventually begins to fear and hate his wife.
*   **Part Three**: Set in the attic of **Thornfield Hall**. Antoinette, now renamed "Bertha," is completely isolated and eventually decides to burn the house down to find her way "home."

**Summary of the Text Excerpt**

In this passage, Antoinette is imprisoned in the attic, guarded by **Grace Poole**. She is deeply disoriented and has lost her sense of time and identity. She describes the world around her as fake and "cardboard," struggling to accept that she is in England because it does not match her vibrant memories of the West Indies. She recalls the traumatic journey and a moment where she was drugged or restrained by a mysterious "**third man**."

**Analysis & Textual Connections**

*   **Loss of Identity**: **"There is no looking-glass here and I don’t know what I am like now"** (lines 1-2). The absence of a mirror symbolizes her loss of self; she can no longer recognize who she has become.
*   **The "Cardboard" Reality**: She repeatedly calls the house a **"cardboard world"** (line 12) or **"cardboard house"** (line 20). This suggests that for her, England is a thin, lifeless, and artificial prison compared to the reality of her homeland.
*   **Alienation**: **"They tell me I am in England but I don't believe them"** (line 13). Her refusal to believe she is in England shows her total psychological disconnection from her surroundings.
*   **Sensory Contrast**: She describes the light in England as having **"no light in it"** (line 13). This contrasts with the intense, colorful Caribbean sun, emphasizing her depression and "grey" existence in the attic.


**Brontë vs. Jean Rhys**

| Feature | Charlotte Brontë (*Jane Eyre*) | Jean Rhys (*Wide Sargasso Sea*) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Perspective** | **Third-person** (Bertha is seen from the outside). | **First-person** (We hear Antoinette's internal thoughts). |
| **Character** | Bertha is a **Gothic monster** ("clothed hyena"). | Antoinette is a **tragic victim** of patriarchy and colonialism. |
| **Rochester** | A romantic, heroic figure seeking redemption. | A **weak, fearful man** who destroys his wife's spirit. |
| **Theme** | Focuses on **morality** and social class in England. | Focuses on **displacement**, identity, and colonial oppression. |

## Aestheticism and decadence

**The birth of the Aesthetic Movement**

*   **Origin and Context**: The movement began in **France** during the last decades of the 19th century, primarily through the ideas of **Théophile Gautier**.
*   **Reaction against the Middle Class**: It was born out of a sense of frustration and uncertainty among artists who reacted against the **materialism** and strict, restrictive moral codes of the **bourgeoisie**.
*   **Art for Art's Sake**: This became the central motto (*L'Art pour l'Art*). Artists withdrew from political and social life into a state of "aesthetic isolation."
*   **The Bohemians**: The "bohémien" represented a form of protest. These individuals led unconventional lives, pursuing intense **sensations**, excess, and the cultivation of art and beauty above all else.


**The English Aesthetic Movement**

*   **Importation to England**: The doctrine reached England thanks to the American painter **James Abbott McNeill Whistler**, who worked there and spread these new ideas.
*   **Roots and Influences**: The movement's origins can be traced back to the Romantic poet **John Keats** and the Pre-Raphaelite artist **Dante Gabriel Rossetti**, both of whom prioritized beauty and art.
*   **Transition Figures**: **John Ruskin** paved the way by searching for beauty in art and life, but it was **Walter Pater** who became the movement's definitive theorist in England.


**The theorist of English Aestheticism**

*   **Main Works**: Pater’s most influential works were *Studies in the History of the Renaissance* (1873) and *Marius the Epicurean* (1885). They were very popular with young people for their subversive messages.
*   **Life as a Work of Art**: Pater rejected traditional religion and argued that **art was the only certainty**. He believed that life should be lived with intense passion, filling every moment with "refined sensations."
*   **The Artist's Role**: The artist was seen as a "transcriber" of their own unique sensations rather than a recorder of objective facts. 
*   **Independence from Morality**: A key implication was that art should have no reference to real life or morality. Therefore, it did not need to be **didactic** (it didn't have to teach a lesson).


**Walter Pater’s influence**

*   **Writers of the 1890s**: Pater’s work deeply impacted authors like **Oscar Wilde** and the members of the "Rhymers’ Club." 
*   **Evolution of the Term**: The term "**decadent**" originally suggested a decline in recognized values, but by the end of the century, it became a proud aesthetic term used across Europe.
*   **Periodicals**: This new taste was famously reflected in *The Yellow Book*, a leading publication of the time.


**The features of Aesthetic works**

Several key characteristics define works belonging to this movement:
*   **Excessive attention to the self**: A focus on individual perspective and ego.
*   **Hedonistic attitude**: A lifestyle or artistic approach centered on pleasure and the senses.
*   **Perversity in subject matter**: Exploring unconventional or "forbidden" topics.
*   **Disenchantment**: A feeling of boredom and detachment from contemporary society.
*   **Evocative language**: The use of refined, suggestive, and highly artistic language.


**The European Decadent Movement**

*   **The French Influence**: In France, Symbolists like **Rimbaud, Verlaine, and Mallarmé** were key. **Joris-Karl Huysmans** wrote the iconic novel *À rebours* (1884).
*   **The Dandy Model**: The hero of *À rebours*, Des Esseintes, became the ultimate model for Wilde’s "dandy," a man who creates an entirely artificial life.
*   **Decadence in Italy**: The movement was represented by **Gabriele D’Annunzio** (especially in his novel *Il piacere*), **Giovanni Pascoli**, and **Guido Gozzano**.
*   **Decadence in Germany**: The poet **Rainer Maria Rilke** was the most notable figure expressing Decadent sensibility in German literature.


**The Dandy: Cultural Insight** (key idea)

*   **Origins of the Term**: The word 'dandy' likely comes from 'Andrew' (a Scottish nickname). It was first popularized by the song *Yankee Doodle Dandy*, sung by British troops during the American Revolution to mock the "odd and ordinary" clothes of American rebels.
*   **The Early Definition**: Originally, it referred to a man who boasted excessively about his appearance, even if his outfit was strange or unimpressive.
*   **George Bryan Brummell (The Leader)**: The dandy evolved into a figure of **vanity, extravagance, and refinement** thanks to Brummell (1778–1840). He became a 19th-century fashion icon known for his exquisite dress and manners.
*   **Brummell's Influence**: He was a close friend and idol of the Prince Regent (later George IV) for 20 years. However, his life ended tragically in a French asylum for the poor after fleeing England due to gambling debts and a quarrel with the Prince.
*   **Dandyism as a Lifestyle**: Brummell transformed dandyism from a way of dressing into a complete **lifestyle**. This trend spread from England to France, where it merged with artistic movements like **Symbolism and Aestheticism**.
*   **The 19th-Century Revival**: Influenced by French culture, dandyism reappeared in England toward the end of the century, personified by the famous figure of **Oscar Wilde**.

## Oscar Wilde

**Life and Works**

*   **Early Life & Education**: Born in **Dublin** in 1854 to a surgeon and a literary woman. He studied at Trinity College and later at **Oxford**, where he excelled in Classics and became known for his **eccentricity**.
*   **Aesthetic Influences**: At Oxford, he was influenced by **John Ruskin** and became a disciple of **Walter Pater**, fully accepting the theory of **"Art for Art’s Sake."** He moved to London in 1878, becoming a celebrity for his wit and his style as a **'dandy'**.
*   **American Tour (1881)**: Wilde conducted a successful speaking tour in the **United States**, where he defined Aestheticism as a "search for the beautiful." His irony and posing made him a famous social figure.
*   **Marriage and Literary Success**: After returning to Europe in 1883, he married **Constance Lloyd** and had two children. His literary talent emerged in the late 1880s with:
    *   **Short Stories**: *The Canterville Ghost*, *The Happy Prince and Other Tales*.
    *   **The Novel**: His only novel, ***The Picture of Dorian Gray*** (1891).
    *   **Drama**: He revived the "comedy of manners" with hits like *Lady Windermere’s Fan* (1892) and his masterpiece, ***The Importance of Being Earnest*** (1895).
*   **Tragedy and Scandal**: In 1895, his relationship with **Lord Alfred Douglas ('Bosie')** led to a trial for **homosexuality**, which was then illegal in Britain. He was sentenced to **two years' hard labour**.
*   **Prison and Exile**: While in prison, he wrote ***De Profundis***, a long letter to Bosie. After his release in 1897, he went into **exile in France**, living in poverty.
*   **Final Years**: His last published work was *The Ballad of Reading Gaol* (1898), written under his prison identity 'C.3.3'. He died of meningitis in a **Paris** hotel in 1900.


**The rebel and the dandy: Oscar Wilde's Aesthetic Ideal**

*   **Life as Art**: Wilde fully embraced the 'aesthetic ideal,' famously stating, **"My life is like a work of art."** He didn't just write about beauty; he lived it.
*   **The Dual Role**: He occupied the double role of **rebel and dandy**. As a dandy, he was an aristocrat whose elegance symbolized the superiority of his spirit.
*   **The Individualist**: Wilde’s dandy was an individualist who demanded absolute freedom and used his sharp **wit** to shock and challenge society.
*   **Ammoral Beauty**: For Wilde, life was meant for pleasure and the pursuit of beauty (in clothes, words, or appearance). Crucially, this interest had **no moral stance**; beauty was an end in itself.
*   **Rejection of Didacticism**: In the 'Preface' to his novel, he claimed: *"There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all."*
*   **Break from Tradition**: With this philosophy, he completely **rejected the didacticism** (the intent to teach a moral lesson) that had defined the Victorian novel during the first half of the century.

### The picture of Dorian Gray

**Plot and Setting**

*   **Setting**: The novel is set in **London** at the **end of the 19th century**.
*   **The Portrait**: The story begins with **Dorian Gray**, a young man of extraordinary beauty, who fascinates the painter **Basil Hallward**. Basil decides to paint a portrait that captures Dorian's perfection.
*   **The Influence of Lord Henry**: Under the influence of the brilliant but corrupt **Lord Henry Wotton**, Dorian abandons himself to a life of pure **pleasure**. 
*   **The Pact**: While Dorian's desires for eternal youth are satisfied, he remains young and beautiful, while the **signs of age, vice, and experience** appear only on the portrait.
*   **A Life of Crime**: Dorian becomes increasingly insensitive and cruel, even letting people die because of his lifestyle. When Basil Hallward eventually sees the **corrupted image** in the portrait, Dorian kills him to hide his secret.
*   **The Tragic End**: Years later, Dorian wishes to change but lacks the courage to confess. He decides to destroy the portrait, which is the only witness to his **spiritual corruption**. 
*   **The Transformation**: By stabbing the canvas, he accidentally **kills himself**. At the moment of his death, the portrait returns to its **original purity**, while Dorian's body becomes that of a 'withered, wrinkled, and loathsome' old man.


**Characters**

*   **Dorian Gray**: He represents the ideal of **youth, beauty, and innocence**. At the beginning of the novel, he is immature and pure, but he is soon influenced by Lord Henry to pursue a life of **hedonism and sensations**. In the end, his vanity and selfishness lead to his ruin, while the portrait becomes the visual representation of the **degradation of his soul**.
*   **Lord Henry Wotton**: An intellectual and a brilliant talker who is extremely **sharp in his criticism** of Victorian institutions, such as marriage and the Church. He acts as a mentor to Dorian, influencing his speech and lifestyle to the point that Dorian begins to mimic Lord Henry's style.
*   **Basil Hallward**: An intellectual artist who falls in love with Dorian’s beauty and innocence. He is afraid to exhibit the portrait because he feels it reveals his **"strange attraction"** to Dorian. He is eventually killed by Dorian, representing the tragic example of an artist destroyed in a **sacrifice for art**.


**Narrative Technique**

*   **Third-person Narrator**: The story is told by an **unobtrusive third-person narrator**.
*   **Internal Perspective**: The perspective is internal, which allows for a process of **identification** between the reader and the character of Dorian.
*   **Sensory Descriptions**: The settings are vividly described using words that **appeal to the senses**, creating a strong atmospheric effect.


**Art for Art’s Sake: Art and Beauty** (key idea)

*   **Cult of Beauty**: Wilde believed that only art, as a cult of beauty, could prevent the **"murder of the soul."** For him, art was not just an aesthetic concept but a spiritual necessity.
*   **Art is Useless**: In the preface to *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, Wilde famously stated, **"All art is quite useless,"** meaning that art should not serve any practical, moral, or social purpose other than its own existence.
*   **Life as Art**: He placed artistic ability above all else and viewed **life itself as a kind of art form**, which must be lived "beautifully" through the pursuit of pleasure and sensations.
*   **The Artist as an Alien**: Wilde saw the artist as an **"alien" in a materialistic world**. The artist writes only to please themselves and is not concerned with communicating theories or moral lessons to others.
*   **The Outcast**: His pursuit of beauty and fulfillment was seen as a **"tragic act"** of a superior being who is inevitably rejected by society as an outcast.


**Allegorical meaning** (key idea)

*   **A Modern Faust**: The story is a 19th-century version of the **Faust legend**. Like Faust, Dorian makes a metaphorical **pact with the devil** (or a wish) to exchange his soul for eternal youth and pleasure.
*   **The Double**: The portrait is not an autonomous entity; it represents **Dorian’s "double"** and his dark side, which he tries to hide and forget by locking it away in a room.
*   **The Moral**: The novel suggests that **every excess must be punished** and that there is no escape from reality. When Dorian tries to destroy the picture, he ultimately faces the punishment for his sins: **death**.
*   **Social Criticism**: The corrupted picture symbolizes the **immorality and bad conscience** of the Victorian middle class, while Dorian’s pure appearance represents **bourgeois hypocrisy**.
*   **Eternity of Art**: At the end, the portrait is restored to its original beauty, illustrating Wilde’s theory that **art is eternal** and survives long after people are gone.

### The painter's studio (128)

**Summary: A Sanctuary of Beauty**

The story opens in the **luxurious and sensory-rich studio** of Basil Hallward. Lord Henry Wotton is relaxing on a divan, smoking "innumerable cigarettes" while observing a **full-length portrait** of an extraordinarily beautiful young man. Lord Henry praises the painting as Basil's "best work" and encourages him to exhibit it at the Grosvenor Gallery. However, Basil refuses, claiming he will never send it anywhere because he has **"put too much of himself into it."**


**Literary Analysis & Textual Connections**

**The Aesthetic Atmosphere**

Wilde uses the setting to establish the principles of **Aestheticism**, where art and beauty are the highest priorities.
*   **Sensory Language**: The studio is filled with the **"rich odour of roses"** and the **"heavy scent of the lilac"** (lines 1-2). This creates a hedonistic, almost overwhelming atmosphere of beauty.
*   **Nature vs. Artifice**: The garden is seen through the window, but it is described through artistic terms like **"momentary Japanese effect"** (line 12), suggesting that for these characters, nature is only valuable when it looks like art.
*   **"The sullen murmur of the bees"**: This personification (line 15) adds to the oppressive, stagnant stillness of the summer afternoon.


**The Philosophy of Lord Henry Wotton**

Lord Henry represents the cynical, witty, and paradoxical voice of the "Dandy."
*   **The Power of Reputation**: He claims, **"There is only one thing in the world worse than being talked about, and that is not being talked about"** (lines 31-32). This highlights his belief that social standing and fame are more important than morality.
*   **Detachment**: He speaks **"languidly"** (line 23), showing his bored, aristocratic distance from the hard work of the world.


**Basil Hallward and the Artist’s Soul**

Basil represents the Romantic idea of the artist who suffers for his creation.
*   **"Skillfully mirrored"**: The text says Basil has mirrored a **"gracious and comely form"** (lines 19-20). He has captured Dorian's physical beauty perfectly.
*   **The Secret Burden**: When Basil says, **"I have put too much of myself into it"** (line 40), he suggests that the painting reveals his own soul or his "strange attraction" to the subject. 
*   **Art as Disclosure**: Basil fears that the portrait exposes his inner feelings, contradicting the Aesthetic rule that art should be "useless" and impersonal.


**The Contrast: Beauty vs. Intellect**

*   **"Beauty ends where an intellectual expression begins"**: Lord Henry argues that intelligence destroys beauty because it makes a face look **"hideous"** or exaggerated (lines 48-50).
*   **The Ideal of the "Adonis"**: Dorian is compared to **Adonis** and **Narcissus** (line 45), mythical figures defined by their extreme beauty and tragic ends, foreshadowing the fate of the protagonist.

## Edgar Allan Poe

**Life**

*   **Early Years**: Edgar Allan Poe was born in **Boston** in 1809. Both his parents were itinerant actors who died of **consumption** when he was very young.
*   **The Allan Family**: Although never officially adopted, he was taken in by **John Allan**, a merchant from Virginia. He attended private schools in England and was considered an exceptional student.
*   **Conflicts & Alcoholism**: In 1826, he enrolled at the **University of Virginia**, but John Allan refused to finance his studies. During this period, Poe started to gamble and began to drink heavily.
*   **Marriage**: After moving between Boston and Baltimore, he settled in Richmond as a **journalist**. In 1835, he married his cousin, **Virginia**, who was only thirteen years old.
*   **Tragic End**: The death of his wife from tuberculosis in 1847 left him heartbroken and worsened his alcoholism. In **1849**, he was found unconscious in the street in Baltimore and died in a hospital a few days later.


**Literary production**

*   **Early Works**: His first book, *Tamerlane and Other Poems* (1827), was totally ignored by critics.
*   **Masterpieces (1840-1841)**: He published *Tales of the Grotesque and Arabesque*. A year later, he wrote ***The Murders in the Rue Morgue***, which is considered the **first detective story** in history.
*   **Fame & Success**: He became famous with *The Gold-Bug* (1843) and achieved immense success with the poem ***The Raven*** (1845), praised for its rhythm and lyrical invention.
*   **Aesthetic Theories**: In 1846, he published the essay *The Philosophy of Composition*, which contains his important aesthetic theories on writing.


**A master of the short story**

*   **Imagination & Logic**: Poe is acclaimed as a genius of **analytical imagination**, a virtuoso of both logic and hallucination.
*   **International Reputation**: While his work was negatively influenced by a moralistic view in America, his reputation grew in Europe thanks to **Baudelaire** and **Mallarmé**, who translated his works.
*   **Narrative Technique**: His frequent use of **first-person narrative** allows the reader to enter directly into the **interior world** of the protagonist’s mind and see through their eyes.
*   **Importance of Conciseness**: For Poe, brevity and conciseness in short stories were essential to maintaining a powerful emotional effect on the reader.


**Tales of Ratiocination and the Grotesque**

*   **Tales of Ratiocination**: Poe invented the detective story genre. His character **C. Auguste Dupin** uses logic and "purely intellectual" methods to solve crimes, anticipating modern detectives like Sherlock Holmes.
*   **Tales of the Grotesque**: These stories focus on the **"inner psychology"** of **"deranged characters"** (line 19). The protagonists often suffer from a **"morbid acuteness of the senses"** (line 21), leading to a loss of contact with reality and a descent into madness.


### The Fall of the House of Usher (1839) (?)

*   **The Story**: The narrator visits his childhood friend, **Roderick Usher**, in a gloomy mansion. Roderick is mentally ill and hypersensitive, while his twin sister, **Madeleine**, suffers from **catalepsy**. 
*   **The Climax**: After Madeleine's apparent death, she is entombed in the house. During a storm, it is revealed she was **"buried alive"** (line 64). She reappears to kill Roderick in a final embrace of terror, and the house collapses into the tarn (the lake).


**Features and Themes (Analysis)**

*   **Blurring of Fantasy and Reality**: The story is told by an unreliable narrator, creating a world where it is hard to distinguish between real events and **hallucinations**.
*   **The "Buried Secret"**: The house serves as a symbol for a **"buried secret"** (line 81) or a repressed memory. The physical decay of the building mirrors the mental and physical decline of the family.
*   **Theme of Doubleness**: The text emphasizes **"doubleness and splitting"** (line 92). The house is reflected in the water of the tarn, and Roderick and Madeleine are **"mirror images"** (line 103) of each other, representing two halves of a single, fragmented personality.
*   **Symbolic Setting**: The house is not just a setting but a character itself. Its windows are like **"vacant, eye-like windows"** (line 42), and its total collapse symbolizes the final destruction of the Usher lineage and psyche.

### The oval portrait

*   **Setting the Scene**: Like many of Poe's stories, the events are seen through the eyes of a narrator in a state of **"incipient delirium."** He is staying in a room full of paintings where the atmosphere is filled with "disturbing events" even before they happen.
*   **The Discovery**: While reading a book that describes the paintings in the room, the narrator moves a candelabrum and reveals a portrait he hadn't noticed before. It is an **oval portrait** of a young girl whose lifelike expression first **fascinates and then horrifies** him.


**The Work of Art: Reality vs. Dream**

*   **Blurred Boundaries**: Poe constructs the tale so that **reality shades imperceptibly into dream**. The narrator’s delirium makes the reader wonder if the oval portrait is a real object or a dream image.
*   **A Story within a Story**: The book the narrator finds contains the history of the portrait, creating a narrative structure where the reader is pulled "inside the frame" of the story.


**Analysis: Art as a Mirror of Death**

*   **The Vampire Allegory**: The story functions as an **allegory of the vampire-like relationship** between art and life. To create the "perfect" work of art, the painter figuratively "sucks the life-blood" from his subject.
*   **The Fatal Coincidence**: There is an absolute coincidence between the **completion of the painting and the girl's death**. As the painter captures her spirit on canvas, he literally steals her life.
*   **Mirror of Death**: For Poe, art is no longer a "mirror of life" but a **"mirror of death."** Only the girl's death can explain the disturbing mixture of fascination and horror in her painted expression.

**Summary: The Story of the Painting**

The text is divided into the narrator's discovery and the "volume" that explains the portrait's history. A young maiden of **"surpassing beauty"** marries a painter who is already obsessed with his **Art**, which becomes her **"rival."** The painter decides to portray his wife in a dark, high turret. As he works for weeks, he becomes **"wild and moody,"** lost in his obsession. He fails to see that as the portrait becomes more lifelike, his wife grows weaker. In the moment he completes the masterpiece and declares it **"Life itself!"**, he turns to his wife only to find that **"She was dead!"**


**Literary Analysis & Textual Connections**

**The "Vampiric" Nature of Art**

Poe presents art not as a tribute to life, but as a parasite that consumes it. 
*   **"Tints... drawn from the cheeks"**: The text explicitly states that the colors on the canvas were literally taken from the woman’s face. This implies a supernatural or metaphorical transfer of essence.
*   **"Stealing the spirit"**: As the painter works with **"burning pleasure,"** he is unaware that he is draining the life-force of his **"humble and obedient"** wife.
*   **The Final Irony**: When the painter shouts, **"This is indeed Life itself!"** (line 89), it is the ultimate Gothic irony; the art has become "alive" only because the human subject has perished.


**Art as a Jealous Rival**

The marriage is depicted as a tragic love triangle between a man, a woman, and a passion.
*   **"Art was her rival"**: The bride fears the **"pallet and brushes"** because they take her husband’s attention away from her. 
*   **"Humble and obedient"**: These words emphasize her role as a Victorian victim. She submits to his **"wild and moody"** ambition even though it leads to her destruction.


**The Setting and Atmosphere**

Poe uses typical Gothic elements to heighten the sense of dread.
*   **"The dark high turret-chamber"**: The isolation of the setting mirrors the painter’s mental isolation and the wife's physical imprisonment.
*   **"Ghistly light"**: The light in the turret is described as unnatural, foreshadowing the deathly transformation of the bride.
*   **"Incipient delirium"**: The narrator's own mental state at the beginning makes the reader question the reality of the story—is the portrait truly supernatural, or is it a hallucination born of his fever?


**Symbolism of the "Double"**

*   **The Portrait vs. The Woman**: The painting is a **"mirror of death."** In Poe's world, a perfect representation of reality is dangerous because it replaces the original. The portrait is the **"double"** that survives while the real person fades away.
